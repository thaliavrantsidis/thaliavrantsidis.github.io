Biography

I am interested broadly in what it means for people to understand and to think well. I am currently studying what people think makes a good explanation, and how this might agree with or deviate from ideal Bayesian reasoning. My previous research focused on stereotyping and social inferences: specifically, using Bayesian models to clarify the ways in which people use and mis-use social group information, as well as how this relates to the moral implications of stereotyping.

I am currently a Postdoctoral Research Associate in Cognitive Science of Values at Princeton University. I work with Dr. Tania Lombrozo in the Concepts and Cognition Lab.

I completed my Ph.D. in psychology at the University of Toronto working with Dr. William Cunningham.

Featured Projects

The Effect of Knowledge about a Group on Perceived Group Variability and Certainty about Stereotype-Based Inferences
Second-hand knowledge about a group (e.g. from other people, the media) - but not a lack of first-hand knowledge - is associated with thinking groups are overly homogeneous, and applying stereotypes overconfidently to group members.
Thalia Vrantsidis, William A. Cunningham

Alternative Facts: Belief Differences Lead to Differences in Moral Judgements of Stereotype Use
People may use stereotypes in part because they think that they merely using an acceptable belief about a group, even while others may see this as an immoral stereotype. These difference views in turn may be driven by differences in people’s other beliefs about the stereotype (e.g. how overgeneralized it is).
